Simplifying cnc to cc

But let’s not call the whole thing off.

Words easily used interchangeably have radically different meanings. Understanding the space between can help us all move towards design better technology — and organisations.

I often hear organisations taking proudly about their “large, complex systems”. A job search for “Java” and “enterprise” is pretty much guaranteed to unearth this phrase.

If, like me, your design values include the principle of do less, I’m pretty sure “large” isn’t a good thing per se.

When you realise the systems in question are more likely to be complicated than complex, these opportunities start to feel like an Admiral Ackbar meme.

What is the difference between complex and complicated and why is it important to make evolve from one to the other?

What does complex really mean?

Consider the game of chess. There are at least 10 to the 120 possible games of chess. That’s 1,000,[…114 more zeros…],000 possibilities. At least

That’s a mind boggling number. You could play chess from cradle to grave and barely scratch the surface.

Now remember there are only six different pieces in a chess set and each of those pieces only has a couple, at most a handful, of moves.

A complex system is made up of simple parts. The beauty is that each part can be understood, operates independently, and has clear interactions with other parts.

The system as a whole can have a staggering range of behaviour. You’ll likely never test all the combinations, but if the parts are autonomous and interactions are healthy then the whole will be much more than the sum of its parts.

This kind of system can scale almost infinitely, even whilst individual parts remain simple to comprehend. Complex systems tend to adapt to unexpected conditions, reacting and evolving with natural pace.

What is complicated?

If you’re familiar with the artwork of Heath Robinson you’ll have a picture of what a complicated system looks like. Lots of intertwined parts that have to work the right way, every time, as one. There’s no autonomy and every piece must be carefully directed and managed to get a result.

Unlike a game of chess, where pieces will naturally be removed as the game progresses, try to pull a piece out of a complicated system and it will likely fail suddenly and catastrophically.

Take the example of a classic cash register. A mechanical system that’s relatively simple. At that scale it’s pretty successful. Now try to scale up to a much more complicated calculation and you’ve created Babbage’s Difference Engine.

Whilst it was masterpiece and a triumph on paper, it was impractical to build. From 1823 it took almost ten years (and a lot of government money) to produce only 1/7th of the proposed solution.

A working version was eventually built, from 1989 to 1991. Still three years in the making, even with vastly improved technology, but over a hundred years too late. The working version can be seen at the Science Museum in London, but slip just one cog out of that machine and calculations will fail.

Complicated, mechanistic systems don’t scale well. They adapt to a changing environment about as quickly as a fish out of water. It takes a vast investment of time, energy and resources just to stand still. More often, by the time the initial version is fit for purpose, the world has moved on and there’s little to show for all the the energy, blood sweat and tears that went into it.

The difference

Complex systems are resilient and adaptive. Given care and curation, they scale well and degrade gracefully when parts break. Complicated systems by contrast are brittle and require ever increasing amounts of effort to keep them healthy as they scale.

A complicated system cannot adapt fast enough beyond a certain scale and cannot ultimately survive

Failure in any part of a complicated system has the potential to halt continuity of the whole. Adapting to change becomes harder as the system gets bigger. In a complex system, failure of a part may degrade performance, but the independence and autonomy of parts and their interactions means the system can adapt at pace.

Think big, inflexible dinosaurs and small, flexible mammals.

Simplicity in systems design

In systems design, whether technology or organisations, the discipline of simplicity is similar to white space or negative space in graphic design.

When it comes to solutions architecture, less boxes in a diagram typically leads to more value, just as a page with fewer words is less crowded and tend to communicate better.

Maximise work not done

Anything that’s not there, or can be removed, is one less thing that can cause you a headache in production, expose you to security vulnerabilities, or just plain be a barrier to change, slowing you down in a world that’s already racing ahead of you.

How do we simplify our design thinking?

Technology: from monolith to microservices

You may have heard talk of monolith and of microservice design.

In the past, systems were built as monoliths. A single, increasingly large, intertwined block of software, riddled with dependencies, where a malfunction in any part would regularly bring the whole thing crashing down. From your Windows updates to national air traffic control systems, complicatedness was de-rigeur, hailed as “best practice” and swallowed countless hours, days, months and years of good peoples’ lives and careers to try and make it work and scale.

belief that a monolith can continue to scale is the technology equivalent of the emperor’s new clothes.

Recently, the idea of microservices has fundamentally altered how technology is designed. Whilst it’s not without difficulties and detractors, I do think the shift is a reflection of a wider evolution in the way humanity thinks about systems. Not just technology, but mains, organisations and human interactions.

I think much of the difficulty people have encountered with microservices comes from mistakenly, or unwittingly, trying to apply the monolith mindset in a microservice context. It’s important to let go of the old paradigm before you can grow into the new.

Organisations: from command-and-control to self-organisation

Some people think I exude a deep and pithy zen that takes time to understand. I do my best not to disabuse people of this notion.

If you’ve spent some time with me, you may know I’m ford of saying “As in tech so in life, as in life so in tech”

The technology world’s mind-shift from monolith to microservices has a parallel expression in the way we’re transforming our thinking about how we design our organisations. As in tech, so in life.

As our word gets bigger and more confusing, the centre cannot hold. The only way to cope — to scale up — is to move from complicated organisations to complex ones. The complicated model of top-down management (value extraction) is giving way to a complex one of self-organisation (value creation).

From Peopleware, the book that first prompted me to question the accepted mindset, to Dan Pink, Brene Brown, Rework, Steve Keil, Frederic Laloux and David Marquet, to name a few, there’s rich thinking — and good evidence — that command-and-control thinking is killing your company. My heroes right now, the Corporate Rebels are unapologetic and prolific on the subject.

Each of these thinkers have echoed and developed my thinking and fueled my exploration as I’ve worked my way towards building human-centred high-performing organisations. When I design and build systems — both technology ones and human ones — this wealth of thinking guides elegantly simple and powerful answers.

The idea of the organisation as an organism (an interconnected system of autonomous parts relating to each other as a collective) rather than as a machine (mechanistic, processed-based, siloed) has emerged to express this shift from complicated (command-and-control) to complex (self-organising). The tide of thinking is rising and the power of transformation is being felt. Make no bones about it: transformation will cause everyone, including you, severe discomfort — on every level. Evolution is hard. Ask not for whom the bell tolls.

Crossing this chasm allows you to not only survive, but thrive on the shores of a new mindset

As Frederic Laloux points out in this talk on Reinventing Organizations, if we think self organisation can’t work at scale, consider the free-market economy. Along with the Internet, it’s amongst the biggest, most complex systems humanity has ever created. It’s curated — interactions are regulated — but no one directly controls the economy. These systems are highly stable. They continuously adapt and don’t stop working when individual parts fail. No system is going to be perfect — and perfection probably isn’t the point. Attempting to control and manage on this scale simply isn’t feasible or desirable.

There are only two economies that do use top down economic control: Cuba and North Korea. You probably don’t want to be them.

In Western Europe, only 10% of employees are engaged at work. This is both an economic and a humanitarian tragedy. Transforming your own and your organisation’s mindset from complicated industrial management toa complex connected and human-centred one is both a business and a moral imperative.

If you want to access the benefits of this shift, your principles and values will need to align and resonate with the parallel transformations going on in other areas of life.

Whether you want to benefit from technology and the Internet, or attract, engage and retain the right talent for your organisation to thrive, transformation starts within.
